Conducting User Testing

Why We Conduct User Testing

Understanding how people actually use our applications is very important to sustained development.

It is very easy to make assumptions about ideal users and ideal use-cases when we are developing something, but those assumptions tend to blow up the second we put our applications in the hands of users.

User testing is best performed in an objective setting, following traditional research ettiquette.

This includes things like restricting bias by acting as a neutral observer. You want to avoid training the user while they are exploring your app.

You can give them a series of actions you want them to perform before you conduct the observation, but you should avoid getting involved or answering questions during the observation.

Data Definitions

Quantitative Data

Quantitative data is data than you can do math with. Just because data is numeric, doesn't necessarily mean you can do math with it. Multiplying your zip code by 8 does not yield a meaningful result.

You'll want to include at least two quantitative metrics in your observation.

This can be things like:
  • Time spent on the application
  • Number of clicks per action
  • Number of questions asked during observation
Qualitative Data

Qualitative data is also known as categorical data.

Qualitative data describes things.

While you cannot do math with it, it gives you insight into the thoughts, feelings, motivations, and demographics of your users.

Include at least 5 qualitative metrics in your observation, with at least 2 of them being demographic.

Examples of demographic data:
  • Age Category (i.e. 18-25, 26-35, etc...)
  • Gender Identity
  • Tech Experience Level (i.e. no experience, some experience, etc...)
  • Racial Identity
Examples of non-demographic data:
  • Rating feature categories ordinally (i.e. Rate: Ease of Use on a scale of 1-5, etc...)
  • Asking for feedback based on the Likert Scale

Eliciting Comments

You should feature at least two fields for user comments.

There is a trick to this.

You can't ask leading questions that would influence potential answers, i.e. "How much do you love the really cool feature I added?"

You can't ask overly-broad questions, i.e. "Do you like the app?"

You also cannot simply put a box at the bottom that says "comments", because comments are universally seen as optional and most people skip them.

Instead ask specific questions that yield actionable answers, like "What features do you feel are missing?" or "Was there anything you had a hard time finding?"

Come up with at least two questions like this.

Taking Action

Using your testing data, identify two changes that you could make to your application that incorporate user feedback.

Make sure you make these changes on your final project, I will look for them.

Deliverables

Design and print a minimum of 3 survey forms with a minimum of 9 questions (2 quantitative, 5 qualitative, 2 comments)

Find 3 users (that are not you) and conduct a survey with each of them.

Create a one page report on user testing that includes:

  • Why you used the metrics you used
  • How you interpreted the results
  • The two changes you intend to make in your application

Again, next Wedesday you will turn in: 3 user testing surveys(completed), and a one page testing summary. All should be printed.
